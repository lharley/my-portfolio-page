Project Background and Context

MorphoTrust's business model -- it's technology and identity services -- was extremely complex. The company combined technically specialized SLR cameras, fingerprint scanners, voice recording devices, and retina and voice scanners with custom device drivers. It then combined the devices into application software for identity application processing, authentication and verification -- facial recognition, fingerprint matching, iris and voice recognition. MorphoTrust was also a trusted partner of federal and state governments and used government databases, such as the FBI criminal database, to do background checks on applicants for identity credentials. MorphoTrust also produced identity credentials at 6 plants located throughout the U.S. These credentials were produced to exacting standards of the U.S. government. The company's clients were police departments, state DMVs, the U.S. State Department, the FAA, banks, airports and security companies.

A prototype mockup of a driver license card reader, which verified that security features were present. The prototype was used during application development. Built into the software were network facilities for communicating with federal and state databases to see if the identity on the license had outstanding criminal or other security issues.

Each of MorphoTrust's client agencies were sold a different customized variation of "the product,' so that by 2013, MorphoTrust was maintaining over 50 product installations. Company leadership wanted to combine all the custom installations into a real product, with a single platform based on a modern UI and a backend based on modern coding practices -- flexible and scalable architecture, resuable components, Restful APIs, JavaSript frameworks, .etc.

Personas and Legacy System Documentation

The MorphoTrust product redesign was begun by traditional business analysts (BAs), technical writers, and the development team. Our UX team was created when management found that protential customers were disatisfied with the initial design concepts. Our UX team was established to address the usability issues, non-modern UI look and feel, and lack of technical innovations in the redesign. The Director of UX was hired, then I was the second person hired and became the UX Lead. The team was eventually built out to 8 team members.

Issues with User Research and Personas

When the UX team started our work, we inherited user research and traditional use cases created by BAs.

The Credential Examiner was the key user of the system in the BAs documentation. This role had the most complicated set of task flows, since there were different types of identity credentials to process. In DMV agencies, for instance, there could be as many as 22 different types of licenses, such as Commercial Driver License, which had numerous federal and state laws that had to be satisfied before a license could be issued.

We assumed that the persona information was sound, because they looked very detailed. As we began our UX design activities, however, we found inconsitencies and gaps. The original persona had a very simple job goal -- to help the applicant. And the persona had only two main psychological attributes -- high engergy and the lack of knowledge about computers. A number of the pain points for this persona involved the lack of devices or devices that ran slow, which were not easy to remedy though software. The persona did not have a clear motivation or focus around which to innovate system features. I didn't expect the BAs to write UX-like scenarios, but the use cases and task flows were very complicated and did not coordinate very well with the simple persona.

I met with the buisness analysts and Subject Matter Experts (SMEs). The SMEs explained that they had all worked at state and federal agencies. They had used themselves and their knowledge of staff for the personas. No actual ethnographic research had been done and no quantitative data had been collected from multiple users. Most important for me, no persona scenarios had been developed, so the personas lacked clear task focus in the context of the proposed new software features. Without the scenarios, it was hard to envision how the proposed new system improved the existing system.

Limited Ethnographic and Quantitative Research

Because of time constraints and the reluctance of leadership to revisit work they felt was already completed, we were limited in what we could do to remedy the lack of good personas. However, after presentations to the project manager and other stackholders, we persuaded leadership to allow members of the UX team to talk with and observe real end users as part of our periodic site visits to present design concepts.

While our ethnogrphic research was limited, is was extremely insightful and helped us write better personas. It also helped us find real user pain points, where we could propose innovations. After analyzing the ethnographic research we began an update of the original personas. In talking with Examiners and their supervisor, the Examiner Lead, we learned the complexities of identity rules and agency task flows from the end users' point of view. It wasn't just that Examiners lacked computer knowledge, which was mentioned by the original personas. They also lacked knowledge of government identity laws and weren't always sure of agency policies. A number of examiners said when they were unsure, they went to their co-workers who had more knowlege. It was important to the examiners to help each other. They all reported that it took lots of training and 4-5 years of service before they felt comfortable with all the details of the work.

After analyzing the ethnographic research we began an update of the original personas. The redefined Credential Examiner is shown. We found attributes and motivations that the original peronas did not list. These helped us see ways to innovate the system.

We found it surprising that none of the Examiners talked about the wait time for applicants or their goal of helping applicants. (The stakeholders of the agency, on the other had, were concerned about applicant wait time and customer service.) What the Examiners talked about was the complexities of the different task flows and the need to avoid mistakes or they would get into trouble. One Examiner said she was worried she might lose her job, if she got another bad report. Another Examiner reported that the malfunctioning scanner caused her to produce poor scans, for which she was reprimanded as a mistake. The concern over mistakes was echoed by the Examiner Lead. It is no wonder agencies have a reputation for slow processing times and poor customer service. Examiners are judged on their accuracy to adhere to agency policies. They are not judged on how many applications they process or how fast.

Additional Personas

The personas for the MorphoTrust product turned out to be yet more extensive. The BAs had originally identified the main user as the Credential Examiner who used the software to process identity credentials. This had always been the focus of MorphoTrust software, so it was natural that the BAs focused there. But there was another key user, the applicant, which they mentioned in some use cases. But a persona was not developed.

After our discussions with agency supervisor staff on site, it became clear that a major pain point for agency stakeholders was satisfying applicant needs. The SMEs were a big help in understanding stakeholder motivation. Most of them had been an Executive or Director at a local service center, so they were actual managers and executives we could interview. They confirmed that agency managers were concerned about wait times. They were sensitive to state legislators' and the public's criticism of poor service and delays. They wanted software to help their staff work faster while not making mistakes. Agency management wanted to reduce workload on agency staff by pushing some applicantion processing tasks directly to the applicant via online services. As one SME said, "Budgets for personnel at governments agencies never seem to cover all the staff that is needed to handle the workload."

Because quantitative data showed two major demographic groups for the general population of applicants, one for Millenials and one for Gen-X-ers, we developed personas to represent each group. We developed a persona for Sylvia - Nurse Applicant, a 23-year- old millenial and Frank - Small Business Owner, a 52-year-old Gen-X-er. The persona for Frank - Small Business Owner is shown.

Yet More Personas

Our ethnogrphic research led to more surprises. Only by talking with agency staff did we learn that one of the responsibilities of agency supervisors was the job of uploading the processed applications at the end of the day. This job function was not identified in the original system documents, but it turned out to be key in understanding MorphoTrust's business model and the larger scope of the software.

When agency staff finish processing an applicantion, the application and supporting documentation are sent to a temporary storage area. At the end of each day, the MorphoTrust software bundles these completed applications and sends them to an evaluation server that determines which MorphoTrust production plant will receive applications for producion.

We knew that MorphoTrust had credential production plants, but we did not know that MorphoTrust identity software sold to agencies included (rudimentary) features for sending processed applications to the production plants. Because software to produce the credentials was a separate product in the MorphoTrust existing product line, the BAs had not included it in the systems documentation for the new platform. In fact, the uploading software was a small utility. It was easy to overlook. Only by talking with several supervisors on site did we realize the significance of the uploading utility.

More Pain Points

The upload process was not completely automated, but was initiated manually by an agency supervior. As we discovered from talking with supervisors, this semi-automated data upload resulted in occasional communication issues, either on the agency end or on the MorphoTrust processing server. Agency servers were usually run on old computers and networking services periodically malfunctioned. We learned from talking to the dev team that MorphoTrust did not have automatic error detection in the upload utility and there were limited error detection features in the receiving server software. This information led us to develop a persona for the Agency Supervisor and the MorphoTrust System Admin. We wrote scenarios that innovated ways for lost communications to be detected, reported, and fixed.

Yet More Personas.

The issues produced by the occasional communication failures between the Agecny and production plants had grave consequences. By contract MorphoTrust had from one to four days to produce a credential and mail it to the applicant. If MorphoTrust did not produce the credential and mail it in the specified time, financial penalties ensued.

MorphTrust maintained a dedicated agency Account Manager whose job was to monitor the the agency and any issues the agency was having with MorphoTrust software, including the upload process. The Account Manager was also responsible for monitoring the production of credentials at the production plants on behalf of the agency. It was the job of the Account Manager to communicate to the agency and to the VP of Plant Operations any issues. The VP of Plant Operations's job was to re-route credential production to other plants if there were a production delay. The Account Manager maintained staff for tech support and fixing system issues.

However, because no errors were detected and no alerts sent, the Account Manager did not always know there was a problem. By talking with Account Managers we found that they had manual and ad hoc processes for detecting issues. For instance they would run a processing report, which showed that credential production had stopped. They could then investigate further to find the problem and fit it. But this approach relied on the Account Manager to suspect a problem and manually run the report.

By this point in the project, the deveopment team and product management were aware of some of the issues the Account Manager faced. Like us, they saw a need for the Account Manager, the Plant Manager, and MorphoTrust executives to be users of the new system. We developed personas and scenarios for these key uers, which led to additional innovations. One of the solutions we proposed was a Dashboard for each key user in the system. The Dashboard provided status information and alerts for all key job tasks, which provided users a single place to monitor their work. MorphoTrust Account Managers were provided access permission to view the dashboards of key agency staff.

The final vision of the personas for the system. We broke the the personas out by groups, based on the organization or company the persona worked for. The groups broadly aligned with the modules on which the proposed new system was built.

Final Personas for the Issuance360 Platform

In the end, the MorphoTrust platform turned out to have a complex set of personas.

We grouped the persons based on the company or ogranization, which made it easier to consider the group as a self-contained unit and to design sytem features dedicated to the needs of the personas within the group, without reference to other groups. By using the groups as a whole, we could think about the relationships between the groups and design features to support these relationships. f
